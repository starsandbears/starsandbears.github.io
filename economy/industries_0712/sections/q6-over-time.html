Question 6Has it always been split like that?
✦

Nope — the slices slowly change shape.

Over 40 years, two slices grew and two shrank. Housing crept up from about 30% to 33% and health care nearly doubled its share (4.8% → 7.9%), while food eased from ~15% to ~13% and the clothes slice shrank by more than half (6% → 2.5%) as clothes got way cheaper. The family budget is its own little pizza — same pizza, different-sized slices.

Each line is one slice's share of family spending, 1984–2024 (BLS CES). Rising lines are eating more of the pizza.

But in real dollars? They shot up. 🚀 The slices only showed how the pizza is cut. Here's the whole pizza in real dollars, sliced into the same categories — about $22,000 per family in 1984, about $78,500 by 2024 (the top of the stack), roughly 3½ times as much — and every slice balloons, even though the shares barely moved.

The actual dollars each year, stacked into the same categories (BLS CES, 1984–2024). The top of the stack is total spending; every slice grows.

Why so many more dollars for the same shopping? Because prices climbed. A family often isn't buying more — it's paying more for the same things. That's inflation — and the CPI (Consumer Price Index) is the ruler grown-ups measure it with.
