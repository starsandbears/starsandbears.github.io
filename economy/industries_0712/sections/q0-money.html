First, the big questionWhere does our money come from?
✦

Money doesn't grow on trees — it gets born!

Dollars are born in two nurseries. The famous one: when the economy needs more money, the Federal Reserve (the Fed) creates brand-new dollars out of thin air. It doesn't just hand them out, though: it trades them to banks for debts, called bonds. That's nursery #1 — and it isn't even the big one!

Nursery #2 is an ordinary bank. When a bank gives someone a loan — say, $100 to open a lemonade stand — it doesn't take that $100 out of anyone's savings. It simply types a brand-new $100 into the borrower's account. Nobody's account goes down; the world just has $100 more. And it's the very same trade the Fed makes — brand-new money swapped for a debt: a promise of good work to come and a trust in the future — it lets tomorrow's lemonade stand open today. The debt the Fed owns is called a bond; the debt the bank owns is called a loan. Most of America's money was born exactly this way — not printed by the Fed, but lent into existence by banks. Put the two nurseries together, and here is money's whole life in one picture:

Two nurseries, one big working life, two ways home. Click the picture to zoom in.

But wait — if paying back erases the $100, what did the borrower pay it back with? Other dollars they earned — money still alive, out doing its circulation. And was the erased $100 💸 wasted? Look around: while it lived, it got traded, hand by hand, into real things — the house that got built, the shop that opened, the skills someone learned.

💸 gonewhen the loan is paid back
🎛️ the Fed's dialinterest rates set the borrowing pace

Note: yes, this is really how it works — see the Bank of England's “Money creation in the modern economy” (2014 Q1).

Money pilesSo with all this borning and vanishing… how much money is alive right now?
✦

About $22 trillion — and look at what all the finished lives left behind.

Count every dollar alive right now — cash in wallets, plus what's in checking and savings accounts — and you get about $22 trillion. Grown-ups call this pile M2.

But the money pile isn't the whole story — remember what dollars do before they vanish. All those finished lifetimes got traded into assets: things that keep their value — houses, businesses, savings set aside for later. Add up everything American families own, subtract what they still owe, and you get about $180 trillion of net worth — eight times the money pile! Dollars come and go; the value they build up stays.

Follow the moneyTwenty-two TRILLION dollars?! Okay… who's actually holding it?
✦

Mostly, it's sitting in families' accounts.

Line up everyone's ready money — wallets, checking, savings and money-market accounts — and nearly two-thirds of it belongs to households like yours — about 63%. Nonprofits — churches, charities, museums, universities — keep about 3%. Businesses big and small hold about a fifth (their payroll and rainy-day cash). Governments keep about 6%. And roughly 7% sits with the rest of the world: people and companies abroad who like saving in dollars.

Who holds America's ready money — cash plus checking, savings & money-market accounts (Fed Z.1 Financial Accounts, Q1 2026). Hover a bar.

But wait — where are the banks? 🏦 Trick question! A deposit is your money and the bank's debt — so banks can't be on the holders' list at all. And the small government slices on the chart are what governments keep at ordinary banks. Governments don't sit on mountains of cash. The mountain of savings is ours.
