Question 3Why are paychecks so different across industries?
✦

Because some work makes a lot of value with very few people.

When we put each layer's share of the cake (value) next to its share of the jobs (people), some really stand out. Information (apps, phone, streaming) bakes about 5.5% of the cake with under 2% of the workers. Real estate is the wildest: the thickest layer, almost no bakers — because a house earns its "rent" just by existing. On the other side, restaurants, stores, and health care need lots and lots of people.

Red = share of the cake · Blue = share of the jobs. A tall red bar with a short blue bar means "big value, few people."

And there's a second part to why paychecks look so different — who keeps the money? A layer's value added is everything its whole team bakes together, and most of it flows right back out: paychecks for the bakers, taxes, worn-out ovens to replace, interest on the loan. Only what's left after every bill is paid belongs to the companies — that's profit.

For a grocery store, profit is about 1 cent of each sales dollar. Across all industries, the average is about 10 cents. Products and services that cost less to make a copy but are hard for others to copy — oftentimes requiring years or even decades of research, investment and commitment — keep more.

Profit: cents the companies keep out of each sales dollar — after paying suppliers, workers, rent and taxes — by type of business. The dashed line is the market average (~10¢).

Two different "keeps"! Value added (Fig. 8) is everything the whole team creates — paychecks included. Profit is only what's left for the companies after every paycheck and bill. High profit comes from things nobody else can copy (a secret recipe, a patent, a brand); low profit comes from selling what everyone else also sells.

One more secret hiding in both charts: every dollar a layer earns — the paychecks and the profit — had to walk in through the front door first. Somebody bought something. Trace any industry's earnings back far enough and you almost always land in the same place: a family choosing to spend. Grown-ups call that the consumer world 🛒 — and after a quick detour to your own state and one more question about what things are really worth, that's exactly where we're headed.
