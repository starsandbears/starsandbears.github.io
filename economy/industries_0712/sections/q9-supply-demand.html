Question 9Those spending slices from Question 6 keep bugging me… clothes kept getting cheaper, but housing and health care only got pricier. Why do some prices fall while others climb?
✦

Every price is a tug-of-war: how much people need a thing vs. how much of it there is.

When lots of families want something scarce — a home in a popular town, a doctor's visit — they end up bidding against each other, and the price climbs. When factories can churn out mountains of something — T-shirts, TVs — supply races ahead and the price falls. That's the secret behind those lines: clothes are easy to make more of; land and doctors aren't. So the cure for a high price is usually build more, not buy more.

Can we see the tug-of-war? 📊 Sort of: count the homes, cars and hospital beds the country has, next to the people who need them — all set to 100 in 2010. A line climbing above the grey "people" line means we made more than enough to go around; slipping below means scarcer per person — and that's where prices bite.

How much there is of each, compared with 2010 (2010 = 100). Grey dashed = people (the "need"). Each line runs to its source's latest year: people & homes 2026, cars 2023, hospital beds 2022. Sources: population & homes (Census / FRED), cars (FHWA), hospital beds (World Bank / OECD).

See it? 🚗 Cars raced ahead of people, so car prices rose gently. 🏠 Homes barely kept step — and they aren't built where everyone wants to live, so in popular cities they stay dear. 🏥 Hospital beds actually shrank while the country grew — a big reason medical prices keep climbing.

"People" is only a rough stand-in for demand — income, cheap borrowing, and where folks want to live matter too. But the picture is real: what we can mass-produce got roomier; what we can't got scarcer per person.

More buyers → ↑demand up, prices up
More supply → ↓build more, prices fall
🏠 Hard to build fast= stays pricey (scarce)
