Where the numbers come from

Same real data as the grown-up report — just told as a bedtime story. All figures from primary US government and academic sources.

  • Where money comes from (the two nurseries): Bank of England, “Money creation in the modern economy” (Quarterly Bulletin 2014 Q1) — bank lending creates deposits; the money-lifecycle picture follows that mechanism.
  • Money piles (M2 & the treasure it left behind): M2 money stock from FRED M2SL (~$22T, annual average); families' net worth (~$180T) from the Fed's Z.1 Financial Accounts, table B.101 (FRED TNWBSHNO).
  • Follow the money (who holds it): Federal Reserve, Z.1 Financial Accounts, holder-side accounts by sector, Q1 2026. Context figures outside M2: bank reserves (FRED WRESBAL, ~$3.1T) and the Treasury General Account (FRED WTREGEN, ~$0.8T).
  • Question 1 (the cake & its layers, 2025): U.S. Bureau of Economic Analysis, GDP by Industry (value added, full-year 2025). How the layers changed, 2005–2025: same BEA series via FRED (quarterly series averaged to annual and divided by GDP) — Manufacturing fell from ~13% to 9.4% while professional/tech, health care, finance and information rose.
  • Question 2 (the bakers: jobs, pay & the poverty line): U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, QCEW — employment and average annual pay per worker by NAICS sector, 2025 (Accommodation & food services $30,935; Information $184,220). Poverty line = 2025 HHS poverty guideline for a family of four, $32,150 (48 contiguous states). Money flowing through industries: BEA Gross Output by Industry via FRED (all-industry gross output ≈$53T, 2025) — M2 is a money stock; gross output and GDP are annual flows.
  • Pick your state (the state picker): GDP by industry by state from BEA SAGDP2 (nominal, 2025); jobs & pay by industry by state from BLS QCEW (2025). Private industries are private-sector; "Government" is federal + state + local combined. State figures may differ slightly from the national charts (the private/government split).
  • Question 3 (profits — cents kept): Aswath Damodaran (NYU Stern), Margins by Sector, Jan 2026 (US-listed firms).
  • Question 4 (what's “worth” — value & taxes): “Value added” = each industry's contribution to GDP (BEA, GDP by Industry). Family paycheck split from the BLS Consumer Expenditure Survey, 2023 — income before taxes $101,805, after taxes $87,869 (income taxes ≈$13,936), spending $77,280. Kept whole-vintage 2023 (not 2024) because BLS paused its tax estimate in the 2024 release — every piece here is a direct published figure, no estimates.
  • Question 5 (the family budget & the $1,000 game): BLS Consumer Expenditure Survey, 2024 ($78,535 average household spending). Consumer spending ≈68% of GDP: BEA NIPA table 1.1.10 (FRED DPCERE1Q156NBEA, 2025 quarterly average).
  • Question 6 (spending over 40 years): BLS Consumer Expenditure Survey historical tables, 1984–2024 — each category's share of spending, and the dollars, per year.
  • Question 7 (spending by age & Engel's law): BLS Consumer Expenditure Survey, 2024 — Table 1300 (by age of reference person) and Table 1101 (quintiles of income before taxes).
  • Question 8 (spending where you live): BEA Personal Consumption Expenditures by State, 2024 — per-person totals (SAPCE2) and category detail (SAPCE3).
  • Question 9 (supply vs. the crowd): population from FRED POPTHM; housing units from the Census Housing Vacancy Survey (FRED ETOTALUSQ176N); registered vehicles from FHWA Highway Statistics MV-1 (through 2023); hospital beds per 1,000 people (including the 1980 ≈6-per-1,000 figure) from the World Bank / OECD (through 2022).
  • Background cake artwork: the 🍰 slices are from Twemoji, © Twitter/X & contributors, licensed CC-BY 4.0.
