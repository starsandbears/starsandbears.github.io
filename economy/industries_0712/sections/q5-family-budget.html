Question 5Wait — did you say two-thirds of the cake comes from us?
✦

Mostly, yes — follow a dollar upstream and you'll usually find a family's wallet.

Look back at the cake: about two-thirds of it — roughly 68¢ of every $1 — is households like yours buying things. Grown-ups call it consumer spending. The shop's money comes from shoppers; the factory's money comes from the shop; the trucker's money comes from the factory. The rest of the cake is businesses building tools for later, governments, and trade with the rest of the world. So the economy's main engine isn't a bank or a boardroom — it's kitchen tables like yours. Welcome to the consumer world. 🛒

Let's zoom into one of those kitchen tables. The average US household spends about $78,500 a year. The biggest bite by far is housing — about a third — then getting around (~17%), food (~13%), money set aside for savings & insurance (~12%), and health care (~8%). The first four bites — home, wheels, food and savings — eat about 74¢ of every dollar.

Every dollar the average family spends, split into slices (BLS Consumer Expenditure Survey, 2024). The five tan slices used to hide inside one big "Everything else" — now you can see what's in it. Hover a slice for the exact dollars.

"Housing" here is the whole home group: rent or mortgage, plus utilities and stuff for the house.

Note: consumer spending ÷ GDP ≈ 68% — 2025 quarterly average, BEA NIPA table 1.1.10 (FRED DPCERE1Q156NBEA). The other shares: business investment, government, and net exports.
