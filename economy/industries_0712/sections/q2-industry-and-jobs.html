Question 2So who bakes all these flavors?
✦

The grown-ups! Baking a layer of this cake is exactly why they go to work every day. Before we meet the bakers, peek inside the kitchen. The layers of the cake are baked on top of each other: every layer buys its ingredients from other layers — the car factory buys steel from the mill, the restaurant buys vegetables from the farm. That chain of layers is a supply chain, and here's the cool part: because everything travels down one before it reaches you, way more than $22 trillion changes hands every year — over $50 trillion! Fig. 7 shows how the money flows from the downstream consumers to the upstream industries. Add up every sale at every stop, across every industry, and you get gross output — all the money flowing through.

But not all of that money is new. Only the new value a layer bakes on top of the ingredients it bought is truly its own — that's value added: a layer's sales minus what it buys from the other layers, so nothing gets counted twice. America's factories all together sell about $7.3 trillion of stuff but buy about $4.4 trillion of metal, chips and parts, so their value added is about $2.9 trillion. Stack up every industry's new value — layer on layer — and you get GDP, the whole cake — Fig. 8 compares the two for every big industry. 🍰

Fig. 7 · $30,000, four stops: hopping backwards up the chain, and a register rings at every hop. Brown arrows = all the money that flowed through (gross output); dark green boxes = the value added kept at each stop.
Fig. 8 · For each industry: the whole river of money flowing through it (gross output, light green) vs. the new value it adds (dark green) — which is its layer of the cake. Wait — how can $53T flow through industries when only $22T of money exists? Because money doesn't get used up when it's spent! The dollar that buys your candy pays the store's cashier, who spends it at the barber, who buys groceries… Each dollar in the M2 pile rings a few different cash registers every year. Money (M2) is a pile; gross output and GDP are flows added up over a whole year — that's why the yearly flows can be much bigger than the pile.

And most of the bakers work on the caring-for-people flavors — not in factories or at computers.

About 156 million people work at America's employers — self-employed folks add several million more. The two biggest groups work in health care (doctors, nurses, dentists, care homes) and for the government (teachers, soldiers, police). Lots more work in stores and restaurants. Way fewer work at the famous "computer" companies than you'd guess.

How many people work in each part of the economy (millions of jobs, 2025). Same 20 flavors as the cake.

These jobs are grouped the exact same way as the cake's layers, so you can compare them fairly in the next question.

But do those jobs pay the same? Not even close. Some of the industries with the most workers — stores, restaurants and care work — are among the lowest paid, while information (tech), finance and company head offices pay two-to-three times more. The dashed line is the poverty line for a family of four (about $32,150 a year): almost every industry's average paycheck clears it — but the average restaurant & hotel paycheck, at $30,935, lands just below.
