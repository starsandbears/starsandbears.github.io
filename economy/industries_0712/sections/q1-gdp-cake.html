Rich yet?So does that giant $22 trillion pile mean our country is rich?
✦

Not really! A country isn't rich because money sits in its piggy bank — it's rich because of the giant cake it bakes every year: GDP.

To measure how rich a country is, we don't count the cash lying around. That pile is just our ready money. The treasure it left behind — net worth — is one answer: what we own. But economists' favorite ruler counts something livelier: all the new stuff and useful work everyone makes in a year (houses built, meals served, apps written, patients cared for). That grand total is Gross Domestic Product (GDP) — about $30.8 trillion for the US, and we picture it as one giant cake to share.

Question 1Then… what are the flavors of the cake?
✦

Great question. The $30.8 trillion cake has 20 layers — each with its own flavor.

Think of every kind of business as one layer of the cake, with its own flavor — housing, health care, food, computers and books. A layer's thickness is how much value it adds. The surprise? The thickest layer isn't toys, or even computers — it's houses and apartments (a flavor we call "real estate"), because everybody needs a place to live.

Has the cake always tasted like this? Nope — the recipe slowly changes. Over the last 20 years the "making things" layer got thinner while the "services & ideas" layers got thicker. Manufacturing slid from about 13% of the cake down to 9%, while professional & tech work, health care and information all climbed.

Each line is one big layer's share of the whole cake, every year from 2005 to 2025. Sliding down = a thinning layer; climbing = a thickening one. Hover a line.

Those are the layers as shares of the cake. In plain dollars, every layer grew, because the whole cake swelled from about $13 trillion in 2005 to $30.8 trillion in 2025. Even manufacturing's thinning share means more dollars than before (about $1.7T → $2.9T) — its layer grew slower than the cake; it didn't shrink. And remember: part of every line's climb is just prices going up over the years (inflation), not only more stuff.
