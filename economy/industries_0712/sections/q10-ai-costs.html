Question 10Everyone keeps talking about AI — could that make things cheaper?
✦

Some things, yes — AI is a super-fast helper that makes more with less.

Think of AI as a tireless helper that can do certain jobs much faster — writing, coding, answering questions, spotting patterns. When you can make more of something with less effort, that's exactly the "more supply" that pulls prices down (remember Question 9). It's the same reason clothes and gadgets got cheap — machines made mountains of them. So for information-y things — apps, help desks, translations, a lot of paperwork — AI could make them cheaper and better.

And here's a catch grown-ups argue about: when AI makes a company's work cheaper, do the savings turn into lower prices (if lots of companies compete) or bigger profits (if only a few do)? Today AI is a powerful and affordable supply booster — it'll make plenty of things cheaper and better. But it's a helper, not magic, so it still relies on humans.

Mostly, yes! But falling prices come in two flavors — one is a gift, the other is a spiral.

Good deflation 🎁 is when prices fall because we can make more, cheaper — like clothes and TVs did (remember Question 6). Your dollar buys more every year; life gets better. If AI floods the world with cheaper goods and services, that's this kind — abundance. Falling prices from plenty are good news, not a crisis.

Bad deflation 🌀 is when prices fall because spending collapses — and it feeds on itself: people wait for lower prices → shops sell less → jobs and pay get cut → even less spending → prices fall further. Debts make it crueler: your loan stays the same size while your paycheck shrinks. That spiral is hard to escape.

So here's the real puzzle about AI: could we turn the gains into income and spending?

A city with an extra "AI mind" that cares. 🌆

Picture your city a little further down this road. Tiny sensors taste the water in every pipe and sniff the air on every block, while AI-assisted manufacturers churn out the low-carbon infrastructure that scrubs that air a little clearer every year; the moment something's off, the city fixes it — before anyone gets a cough. A health helper in every pocket keeps a gentle eye on grandpa's heart — and a billion other hearts at the same time — and waves a human doctor over at the first strange beat: the kind of everyone-gets-watched-over personalized care that isn't possible right now. A patient tutor for every kid that never runs out of new ways to explain fractions. Rides that look after their riders — walking a kid safely to school, kneeling for a senior, greeting a visitor in their own language. Smart power grids that save the windy night's electricity for the hot afternoon. Farms that give each plant exactly the sip it asks for, with AI keeping watch on the soil itself — the quiet pantry all our nutrients come from. Buoys and gliders listening to the sea — the cradle of our seafood and a treasure chest of climate clues. Scientists with AI microscopes hunting cures in months instead of decades. What's more, it empowers us to pursue our interests and creativity — helping open up whole new industries and markets that make the cake richer.
