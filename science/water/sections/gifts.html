Gifts in the water

One more look — we can choose to be aware of the water. And that awareness is where our freedom begins. The water is what feeds generations of us, lifts us all, and supports our swimming.

There is a whale grandfather in it, who swims slow and enormous above the little ones, keeping them shaded from the drift of plastics. There is an octopus mother's handwriting — eight pens going at once, in ink he made himself — labeling every jar in the reef for whoever comes along hungry next. The jellyfish sisters — a slow galaxy of soft bells — hang their lanterns through the dark water, so that no depth is ever entirely without light. The seal uncles swim their rounds day in and day out, skimming the water clean and keeping it sweet for everyone downstream to breathe. The sea star kiddos leave games lying on the sand — rules unwritten, five-pointed, plainly meant to be found. And the coral great-great-grandmothers built the house itself, room by patient room, and never once saw it finished — the way languages were built, and cities, and laws, and the accumulated sciences. Behind them all runs the sea's oldest habit: abundance. The sea has never given one answer to any question — ask what a home should look like, and it answers with ten thousand shapes of coral in ten thousand colors. Whatever works, the water keeps.

The influence in that ordinary Thursday was never a thing anyone could point to — it was dissolved in seawater like sugar and salt: nowhere in particular, yet everywhere in the flavor — which sometimes makes the little ones want more. Kids' appetite, the fuel; flavors, the lure. Together the duo have kept the sea growing: flavors feed the appetite, and the appetite feeds new flavors into being. And when a flavor fails — too bitter to keep — the seals are on their way, with the downstream fish alongside them.

The water is a gift. The mother tongue; the sense of what is warm to say; the norm of giving way to pedestrians; the way we picture a rainy night behind a stranger's clumsiness, so that it reads as human; and so on. These are all gifts passed along from earlier waters. And they are also a kindness: the traditions and the defaults answer a day's ten thousand small questions, so that nobody builds a Thursday from scratch. And freedom from choosing everything is what leaves room to choose what matters. Then the first honest feeling about the water, before any test of its temperature, is gratitude — and awe of the laws of nature.
