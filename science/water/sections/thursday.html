The water

Look around

An ordinary Thursday

Consider a Thursday — that most unassuming of days, which nobody has ever entirely got the hang of. Someone wakes and thinks a few unremarkable thoughts. They dress for work without consulting anyone; the outfit simply looks right. At a checkup, the doctor calls the procedure routine — ninety percent of patients come through fine — and the consent form is signed with the mild serenity of a person hearing good news. At the license counter, a small box about organ donation is ticked, the way ticked boxes do. Dinner is the kind the family has always had, cooked from the usual things that leapt conveniently to hand at the supermarket. A feed scrolls by, showing the evening's world. Sleep.

It was, by every reasonable measure, a thoroughly ordinary Thursday.

Then the camera pulls slowly back — out through the kitchen window, over the rooftops, past the shoreline — and tips downward, rolling beneath the surface. The sound goes soft. Light comes down in shafts. And from below, the same Thursday plays slowly, again. The words the morning's thoughts arrived in were a childhood inheritance: a mother tongue, warmed first in a mother's tone, carried in the way a family talks — and with it, the first shapes a thought could take. The outfit "simply looked right" because the hoodie is the de facto professional look — an unwritten yet durable rule everybody knows. The doctor said "90% survive" not "10% die" — the same fact wearing two different hats — and the signature came easier for it. The little box stayed ticked because some default had to be chosen — print the form ticked and a nation quietly donates; print it empty and a nation quietly doesn't — and whole national organ-donation rates swing on that choice Johnson & Goldstein 2003. Dinner was what a family passes down as normal to want — cooked from the usual things, which sat at eye level on the supermarket shelf. And the feed showed what the evening's world looked like.

Nothing happened. Nobody influenced anybody.

That, all of it — language, habits, shelves, framings, defaults, feeds — is the influence: the medium everyone lives in, like gravity, like weather, like water. Which raises the next question: if every choice is made in water, could the water hold still?
