Look around

There is no neutral water

The research's answer is no — “there is no such thing as a ‘neutral’ design”, say the nudge's own inventors Thaler & Sunstein 2008. According to Vallier 2016, the field has made its peace with the fact that there is no such thing as a perfectly neutral voice, saying perfectly neutral things, from a perfectly neutral place. Even "choice architecture" Wikipedia carries influence; even silence does. And the debate on whether "'nudging' Wikipedia vitiates personal autonomy" is where the field's arguing energy goes Schmidt & Engelen 2020.

The philosophers of influence agree, and then — being philosophers — draw the one difficult conclusion that changes everything. If influence is everywhere, then what makes a decision yours cannot possibly be the absence of influence:

“Since we are never totally free of outside influence, what gives us (part) authorship over our own actions is that we regard our own reasons for acting as authoritative. Manipulation thwarts that.”

— Susser, Roessler & Nissenbaum, Online Manipulation (2019)

Here is the relief inside that conclusion. If everything influences, then influencing someone was never the wrong. Manipulation names something much narrower:

“The salient issue, to our minds, is not whether the influence appeals to the target's rational faculties, but, rather, whether it appeals to their conscious decision-making process. Which is to say, the issue for us is not rationality but awareness.”

— Susser, Roessler & Nissenbaum, Online Manipulation (2019)

Note also that the self doing the deciding was never a hermit on a rock. The same authors build on relational autonomy — in their words, at full length:

“But let us steer clear of two possible misunderstandings: first, autonomy is sometimes still understood entirely in terms of solipsistic ‘rational choosers,’ without taking social contexts (and social others) into account. We agree with criticisms of this conception of autonomy and develop our own conception from theories of relational autonomy. Such accounts understand autonomy as a capacity of socially-situated persons, whose decisions issue from deliberative processes rich with emotion, feeling, imagination, and reason, and which are conditioned in part by their contexts.”

— Susser, Roessler & Nissenbaum, Online Manipulation (2019); developed further in Susser, Roessler & Nissenbaum, Technology, Autonomy, and Manipulation (2019)

And folded inside that careful philosophy is a warmer discovery: the water, too, is rich with emotion, feeling, imagination, and reason. The self lives on what the currents carry — and among everything they bring, always, the blessing, the compassion, and the connection to others.
